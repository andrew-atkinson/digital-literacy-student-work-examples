Project 1 · Project Brief · Spring 2024

Counterfactual History

PHOTOSHOP · ARFD 106 DIGITAL LITERACY

This is your chance to show off your new digital skills and flex your creative muscles, too. You are to create a layered Photoshop image that explores either counterfactual history or speculative fiction. A kind of Time Machine if you like.

Counterfactual History

Look into moments in history that you might want to change (these moments will likely be after the advent of photographs, but needn't be). Find an event in history and imagine different outcomes for it. What would have changed, and how can you show it visually? Frequently these events are military, medical, religious, scientific or other macro historical events, but needn't be. What if Picasso had rejected art (but Braque and Juan Gris still became artists and met)? Many TV shows, movies and novels are based upon this idea — I'm sure that you're familiar with some.

Speculative Fiction

Speculative fiction is related but looks in the other direction — typically to the future, but not always. The subject speculates on humanity's future: the colonization of Mars, increasing wealth disparity, our sedentary futures; on the planet and biology: environmental disasters, hypothetical technologies. Some artists even imagine people colonizing an errant iceberg or, through an unmade film, provide the seed ideas for many Sci Fi classics.

What to Create?

What characterizes the successful projects is the depth and thoroughness of the speculations — take time to talk over your ideas with a friend or classmate (use your groups to bounce ideas off, you can use the discussion board for this project to do this). Don't just think of the event itself, but also through the possible ramifications.

The final image could be photographic, more drawn, more graphic in its nature. This is open. You could make a fake photo; an advert of a product that changed everything (you could even make it into the illusion of a billboard for example); it could be more drawn like a graphic novel.

What to Use?

You have the internet at your disposal — resources like Pixabay, the Met, or Google Arts & Culture, can provide a lot of imagery, but that doesn't mean that you can't use your own.

What to Submit?

You should submit a PSD file that shows some speculative fiction or counterfactual history. Photoshop is particularly good at creating things that could be real.

The file should have:

  • At least 3 layers.
  • At least two content layers (images / text); the layers should be functionally necessary to the image.
  • At least one adjustment layer with a significant alteration (e.g. you could shift a hue from red to blue, or place a curve layer with a mask to change the lighting in a room).
  • You should practice non-destructive editing where you can.

The image should show:

  • A clear speculative or counterfactual idea.
  • Depth in the speculation and thoroughness.
  • A convincing illusion — whether this is photographic, drawn or whatever.
  • Craft in how Photoshop is used.

The project should take:

Approximately 9 hours (i.e. a full week of work towards a course).

Peer Reviews

After you have submitted your work you will be asked to review your peers' works. Consider their works from the perspective of technical Photoshop skills and in terms of the imagination in their new worlds.

Prof Reviews

You can, and probably should, schedule some time to meet with me and talk about the work either from a technical standpoint — software relative, visual/composition relative, or, narrative/concept perspective. Most conversations tie all three together, because all three are tied together in the work. You either come to the digital clinic hours, or, make an appointment with me through youcanbook.me. Details for both are on the announcement page.
