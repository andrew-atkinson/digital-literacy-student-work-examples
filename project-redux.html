Project 3 · Final Project Brief · Spring 2024

Redux

AFTER EFFECTS · ARFD 106 DIGITAL LITERACY

Redux means to bring back, or happen again. The creative process is always a redux. There's always ideas that are opening, that need to be found or explored some more. This is the creative life — it is how projects grow and change.

Returning to Earlier Work

Having spoken to most of you it seems there is more life to be found in your existing projects, in re-examining your work on the attention economy, on race and technology, or the fictions and counterfactuals in photography.

There are still ideas to be developed, visual solutions to be explored, and, with the help of After Effects, to introduce time as a new element.

About After Effects

After Effects is an interesting program — it does a lot of things but not without input. It needs vector images, text, photos and video, to work. You have been developing those images and ideas. I think this is a good time to look at them again.

Time as a Material

Time is different, and quite complex. But at the same time you all experience it all the time: you see it in UX transitions, TV titles, in animation, video game openings, news infographics, Google doodles…

But in order to turn your experience into something usable you need to watch them carefully, observe how the movement creates different emotional tones, suggests ideas with the use of time, creates narratives and metaphors in fractions of a second. Watch some that grab you: BoJack Horseman, anyone?

Steal! Great Artists Steal!*

Here are artists who explore aspects of time, some slow some fast, some draw on identity issues, others on politics, some lean on figuration and pop culture, some more abstract and interest in more philosophical ideas. Many are ideas to bounce off of. Regardless, they are all grist for the mill, they're artists to examine. Most important: steal from them; learn from them.

  • Alex Da Corte — makes short videos that evoke kid's tv.
  • Stan Douglas — makes looping and endlessly variation (takeaway: narratives can return to the beginning).
  • Jacolby Satterwhite — leverages afro-futurism and simple video games (takeaway: there are other worlds to draw from!), and Cao Fei did the same for Second Life, many years ago.
  • Matthew Ritchie — used a drawn animation to visualise a drawn architectural environment that is another way to explore his world creation myths.
  • Christian Marclay — works with found footage to create a piece of work 24 hours long, with 24 hours of shots with clocks in them from Hollywood at the right time.
  • Mary Reid Kelley — takes a cartoon language, paints it on herself, and uses that to create absurd videos (takeaway: video and drawing can combine in different ways), so does William Kentridge with cut-paper to make a magical animation/lecture.
  • Paul Pfeiffer — reframes sports footage; sometimes perfectly centering the camera on the basketball making the players revolve around it, or leaving it spinning in the air like a perfect moment.
  • Masim Bagdy — uses color processing to make short poetic movies.
  • Jenny Holzer — has been using animated text to create odd aphorisms that sound like they're about modern life (takeaway: where you put text changes the meaning — use the context).
  • In the 1970s, Beryl Korot put together weaving with screens and emergent digital technologies.

Submission

  • Create a 5–15 second piece that explores new ideas from a previous work. You should introduce new elements, rethink the compositions, and focus a lot on the rhythm and the transitions.
  • Submit a 720p h.264 .mp4 movie.

* Here's the better quote from T. S. Eliot: "Immature poets imitate; mature poets steal; bad poets deface what they take, and good poets make it into something better."
